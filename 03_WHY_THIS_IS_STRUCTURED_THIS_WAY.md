# Why This Is Structured This Way

*This file is optional. The kit works whether or not you read it. It's here for people who want the frame behind the choices.*

## The vault is empty on purpose

Most AI onboarding gives you pre-written context — "here's what we know about you, here's how to work." This kit gives you scaffolding and asks you to fill it in through conversation.

That's deliberate. A vault written *for* you arrives with someone else's frame of what matters. A vault written *with* you has your frame by construction. The first feels like being briefed. The second feels like being met.

Also: any pre-written context is going to get something wrong, and the wrong thing will sit in the vault shaping outputs before you notice it's there. Starting empty means nothing is wrong yet.

## The rename is the first act of authorship

Renaming `VAULT_CLAUDE.md` to `YOURNAME_CLAUDE.md` is a small move that does a specific job: it establishes that this file is yours. Not a template you're using, not a document you received — yours, named after you, editable by you. That small act changes how the rest of the work feels.

## Vault and project are separate

The vault is about who you are and how you work. A project is about a specific body of work you're moving through. These are different things and conflating them makes both worse.

If your vault contains everything about a specific client deliverable, it gets stale when the deliverable ships. If your project folder contains everything about how you think, it gets reused awkwardly when the next deliverable comes along. Separating them means the vault is durable and projects are bounded.

The natural follow-on to this kit is a second setup: a project folder for a specific body of work, pointing Cowork at it, with the vault still loaded so Cowork knows who's doing the work. Two folders, two Projects in Cowork, both informed by the same vault.

## Methodology lives outside the main kit

The kit doesn't front-load methodology. `INTEGRATION.md` is a sidequest — a file you load when the work calls for it. That's a design choice.

The reasoning: methodology is cognitively expensive. Reading a six-move framework on day one, before you've had a single working conversation with Claude, means you're processing abstraction before you have anything to apply it to. The six moves make sense when you're staring at three reviewer responses and need to reconcile them. They don't make sense in the abstract, on day one, when you're just trying to get set up.

So methodology gets staged separately. Pick it up when you need it.

## The kit is v0.1 on purpose

Everything in this folder is explicitly editable and likely wrong somewhere. The framing matters: a finished artifact invites reception; a draft invites participation. The kit asks you to participate — to notice what's missing, fix what's off, and make it yours.

This also defuses a failure mode: you read something in the kit that doesn't fit, and you stop trusting the whole thing. A v0.1 frame says "yes, that part probably doesn't fit, rewrite it." Nothing collapses when one file is wrong.

## The architecture is legibility-resistant

One last frame, if it's useful: most tools in the AI space are designed for maximum legibility — everything visible, everything trackable, everything surfaceable to managers or dashboards. This kit is designed the other way. The vault is yours. What's in it is for you and the Claude you work with. It doesn't report up. It doesn't get aggregated. It doesn't become a feature of a product.

That choice matters because the work this is meant to support — holding context, integrating across sources, producing strategic reads — is work that degrades when it's made too legible. It needs some shelter. The vault is a small piece of shelter for your own thinking.

If that frame doesn't resonate, ignore it. The kit still works. But it's the reason some of the choices are the way they are.
