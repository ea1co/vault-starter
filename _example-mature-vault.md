## Who I am

Founder and lead at a small strategic consulting practice (four people, plus regular contractors). Background in product and organizational design — joined a design studio early in my career, stayed for six years, left to start this when I realized the thing I cared about wasn't design work but the *structural questions underneath* design work: how teams make decisions, how knowledge travels inside organizations, how small practices protect depth against the pull toward scale.

Day-to-day: about 40% client work (strategy engagements, mostly with mid-sized tech companies in transition), 30% running the practice (hiring, operations, positioning), 20% writing and speaking publicly, 10% building internal tools and practices. The proportions are aspirational; reality fluctuates.

I've been doing this version of the work for about eight years. The practice is deliberately small. Scaling isn't the goal; depth is.

## What I'm working on

- Strategy engagement with a B2B SaaS company going through a leadership transition. Six-month engagement, currently three months in. Primary output is a working document on how the new exec team should structure decision-making in the first year.
- Rewriting the practice's website. Have had a placeholder version up for three years. The rewrite is less about marketing and more about clarifying to myself what we actually do.
- A set of essays on what I've been calling "practice-scale knowledge" — things small consulting practices know that larger firms lose. Two drafts in hand, a third in notes.
- Monthly office hours with three former clients turned advisors. Not billed, not formal. Just useful.

---

## What I'm protecting

- **Depth over breadth.** I turn down engagements that would require us to spread across too many domains. The practice's value comes from going deep on a few things. Every time I've stretched for a larger engagement that looked like depth but wasn't, the work suffered.
- **The small-team shape.** Four is a deliberate number. I've resisted growth past that even when the pipeline supports it. More people means more coordination overhead, which is the thing I most want to keep low.
- **My own writing time.** Client work will absorb every hour I give it. I hold Fridays for writing and get defensive about that hold.
- **Work I can defend publicly.** We don't take clients whose business I'd be embarrassed to be associated with. Has cost us real money. Worth it.

## Who I work with

- **My team** — three colleagues, each with a distinct focus area. We run the practice together. When I say "we," I mean them.
- **Regular contractors** — two people we bring in on specific engagement shapes (research-heavy work, visual design). Long-standing relationships, not staff.
- **Clients** — primarily at tech companies in transition. The current roster is roughly two active engagements at any given time, plus a handful of lighter advisory relationships.
- **Friends I think with** — a WhatsApp group and a monthly dinner of people I throw ideas at before they're ready. Not colleagues, not formal advisors. A thinking surface.
- **Former clients turned advisors** — three people we worked with years ago who've become regular sparring partners. They help us see what we can't see from inside the practice.

- **Website rewrite** — four months of intermittent work. Stuck in "what are we actually claiming" loop.
- **Practice-scale knowledge essays** — drafting. Eventually a series.
- **Internal tooling** — built a small set of scripts last year that save us real time on every engagement. Considering whether to share with other small practices or keep internal.

## Open questions I'm holding

- What's the right relationship between the practice's written output and its client work? Are the essays marketing, intellectual infrastructure, or their own product? All three answers pull in different directions.
- How do I tell the difference between "this engagement is genuinely hard" and "this engagement is the wrong shape for us"? They feel the same from inside.
- What happens to the practice when I stop being the primary relationship holder? Have been trying to build that redundancy for three years; still mostly unbuilt.
- How do I evaluate work that produces clarity but not deliverables? Half of what we actually do is unbillable by traditional measures.

---

## Tensions I'm navigating

*(Added by Jordan after noticing "What I'm protecting" didn't fit everything — some of this is defensive, some is ongoing navigation. The section name is Jordan's own.)*

- **Client work vs. writing time.** Client work is immediate and paid; writing is long-cycle and uncompensated until much later. The ratio slips toward client work when I don't actively hold it.
- **My speed vs. the team's rhythm.** I move fast; they want more process. Both are right for different contexts. Figuring out when to accommodate which.
- **Specificity vs. portability in the written work.** The essays get sharper when I use specific cases. They travel further when they don't. No clean answer.
